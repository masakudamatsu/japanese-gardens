Ryoan-ji zen garden

My previous two visits to the most famous zen garden left me unsure of what's really great about the garden. Upon my third visit, I learned from a book on Japanese gardens that this garden must be viewed from just halfway between the left and right edges of the garden. I sat down on the garden-viewing terrace at that viewpoint and contemplated for a while. And I reached nirvana.

Emphasis on Nothing

The garden is all about the composition of fifteen rocks within a rectangular space filled with white gravels. The composition's theme is the emphasis on nothing, echoing the Buddhism's most important teaching of shedding your desire.

Look straight ahead

When I look straight ahead, there is no stone at the center of gaze. The composition is split between two groups of stones on the right and on the left. Nothing is really emphasised.

Look to the left

When I look to the left, there is again nothing between me and the corner of the garden. The view is split. Nothing is emphasised.

Look to the right

Looking to the right from the viewpoint, I see three groups of rocks. But there is none of them between where I am and the corner of the garden.

Five sets of rocks

The 15 rocks of the garden are grouped into five sets. The middle three sets lack the focus while the other two groups, having a symmetric composition, are marginalized in this garden.

The middle-left

The middle-left set consists of two rocks. There is no center. The right elongated one dominates, but it is located to the right side, not at the center.

The middle

The middle set consists of three rocks. One on the left dominates, but it is again not located at the center but to the side.

The middle-right

The middle-right set consists of two rocks, each in a peculiar shape. But neither is emphasized more than the other.

The leftmost

The group of rocks furthest to the the left is the most magnificent composition in this garden if seen from the front of it. One large, well-shaped rock stands in the middle, flanked by two small standing rocks and two flat rocks farther away. But the whole group is located at the left edge of the garden, not at the center.

The rightmost

The group of rocks furthest to the right is made of three rocks if seen from its front. One large lying stone is flanked by two flat stones. This symmetric composition, however, is also located at the edge of the garden, not in the center.

Unity as a whole

The composition of no emphasis, however, makes those who haven't reached nirvana feel uneasy. There needs to be an element of serenity to accept the composition. The Ryoan-ji zen garden achieves this by uniting the five groups of rocks with the similarity of rock shapes.

Pairs of flat stones

The leftmost group of rocks is connected to the rightmost set by the pairs of flat flanking stones.

Squarish standing stones

The leftmost group of rocks is also connected to the middle one by the squarish standing stones.

Lying rocks

The middle left, the middle right, and the rightmost groups of stones are all connected by the shape of lying rocks that look like the back of beasts).

End result: serenity

The garden manages both to place no emphasis and to achieve the overall unity, creating the serenity that zen aims to preach. The key for living a life is not to be preoccupied with one particular thing. It is so easy to forget this simple principle in daily life. The Ryoan-ji zen garden has been there since the 16th century to remind us of an ideal way of life.

Tips for your visit

Arrive when the gate opens (8am from March to November, 8:30am from December to February). Sit down in the middle of the viewing platform. Observe the way the morning sunlight illuminates the garden changes as the time passes by. When a flock of tourists start arriving and counting how many rocks there are, leave and take a stroll around the pond garden outside. Ryoan-ji temple's official website explains how to get there.
